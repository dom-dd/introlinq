The B2B SaaS Growth Playbook: From First Customers to 100,000 Users

Getting a B2B SaaS company from zero to its first 100 customers is a founder problem. Getting it from 100 to 100,000 is a growth problem - one that requires deliberate strategy, the right team, and a clear understanding of which channels will scale. Most founders who hit the first milestone think the second will follow naturally. It rarely does. Here's the playbook.

Product-led growth vs sales-led growth: choosing your motion

The most important strategic decision in early-stage B2B SaaS is whether to lead with product or with sales. Product-led growth (PLG) - where users can sign up, experience value, and upgrade without ever talking to a salesperson - works best when you're selling to individuals or small teams, where the decision to buy is made by the user rather than a procurement committee.

Sales-led growth makes more sense when you're selling to enterprises, when your product requires custom implementation, or when the annual contract value is high enough to justify a human touch. Many successful SaaS companies run a hybrid motion - PLG for inbound and smaller accounts, sales-assisted for larger deals. Getting the balance right in your specific market is one of the most valuable things an experienced growth leader can help you figure out.

Content marketing and SEO as long-term acquisition channels

For most B2B SaaS companies, organic search is the highest-ROI acquisition channel over a two to five year horizon - but it takes time to compound. The teams that invest in content and SEO early tend to see their cost of customer acquisition decline significantly as the business matures, while those who rely entirely on paid channels often find that rising CPCs erode their margins as they scale.

Effective B2B content marketing isn't about publishing thought leadership that nobody reads. It's about creating content that your ideal customer is actively searching for, written to rank in search results and to convert readers into trial users or demo requests. This requires understanding search intent deeply, building topic clusters rather than one-off posts, and optimising systematically for both rankings and conversion.

Building a demand generation engine

Demand generation in B2B is the practice of creating and capturing demand across the entire funnel - from the prospects who don't know they have a problem yet, to those actively evaluating vendors, to those ready to buy. The mistake most early-stage SaaS teams make is investing only in the bottom of the funnel (demo requests, free trials) without building the top-of-funnel awareness that keeps their pipeline full.

Account-based marketing (ABM) has become a powerful tool for B2B companies targeting a defined set of accounts. Instead of casting a wide net and hoping the right companies find you, ABM identifies your ideal prospects in advance and orchestrates coordinated outreach across paid media, content, events, and sales. The result is higher conversion rates and shorter sales cycles for the accounts that matter most.

Positioning and messaging: the foundation of everything

Most B2B SaaS companies underinvest in positioning. They can describe what their product does - features, integrations, pricing tiers - but they struggle to articulate why it matters to a specific customer with a specific problem in a way that makes them feel understood. Good positioning is not a tagline. It's a clear articulation of who you serve, what you do for them, and why you're different from every other option they're considering.

Product marketing is the discipline that connects your product to your market. A strong product marketer translates the capabilities of your engineering team into the language your buyers use when they describe their problems. They also ensure that positioning is consistent across every customer touchpoint - from paid ads to sales decks to onboarding emails to the product itself.

International expansion: timing and sequencing

Most SaaS founders expand internationally too early, before they've fully saturated their home market or understood what makes their existing customers successful. The temptation to expand is understandable - international revenue feels like growth - but it comes with significant complexity: localisation, legal entities, local payment methods, customer success capacity across time zones, and often a significant slowdown in the core business while leadership attention is divided.

The companies that expand successfully tend to do so because international demand is already pulling them - customers in new markets are signing up without the company actively selling there. When expansion is demand-led rather than strategy-led, the company is far more likely to find product-market fit quickly in the new geography.

Hiring your first marketing leadership

The transition from founder-led marketing to professional marketing leadership is one of the most consequential hires in a SaaS company's journey. Hire too early and you get an expensive person running experiments without enough data to inform decisions. Hire too late and you leave growth on the table while competitors outpace you in channels you're ignoring.

The best early marketing leaders for B2B SaaS are typically people who have operated in a similar stage company, understand both strategy and execution, and can work across content, demand generation, and product marketing. A fractional CMO can be a powerful option in the period between founder-led and full-time hire, giving you senior strategic guidance without a full-time salary commitment.
